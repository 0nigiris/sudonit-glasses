## 1. What is Sudonit?

Sudonit is a small smart-glasses module that **clips onto the glasses you already own.** It's
not a new pair of glasses — it's a lightweight pod with a camera, a speaker, Wi‑Fi, a tiny
computer, and a button that attaches to the arm of your existing frames and makes them smart.

Look at something, press the button, and a voice in your ear tells you about it. And even with
your phone in another room, the device is still alive — it knows the time and keeps its
settings on its own.

- **What it is:** a clip-on module that turns the glasses you already wear into smart glasses.
- **Who it's for today:** makers, tinkerers, accessibility hobbyists, and people who want an
  open device they truly control. Over time: enthusiasts, and eventually everyday wearers.
- **Why it exists:** because the glasses on your face should be **yours** — open to repair,
  modify, and own — not a sealed gadget locked to one company's frames, cloud, and AI.

No jargon version: **keep your glasses, add a brain you own.**

## 2. Elevator Pitch

**One sentence.**
Sudonit is an open, repairable smart-glasses module you clip onto the frames you already own —
your hardware, your glasses, your choice of AI.

**30 seconds.**
Most smart glasses make you buy *their* frames, run *their* cloud, and accept *their* rules —
a sealed box you can't repair or really own. Sudonit flips that. It's a small module that clips
onto the glasses you already wear. Look at something, press a button, and hear an AI answer in
your ear. You own the hardware, you can fix and upgrade it, you can move it between your
favorite frames, and you choose who runs the AI — your own key, a hosted Sudonit service, or
your own server. It's the ThinkPad philosophy for smart glasses.

**2 minutes.**
Smart glasses are arriving fast, but the ones on sale are closed products: beautiful, sealed,
and not yours. You buy the company's frames, your data flows through the company's cloud, the
AI is the company's choice, and when something breaks — or the company moves on — you're left
with a gadget you can't open, fix, or change. Glasses are deeply personal; people wear frames
they love every day. You shouldn't have to give that up to get a little intelligence.
Sudonit is built on a different idea, borrowed from the ThinkPad: a device that is open,
modular, repairable, and genuinely owned by the person wearing it. Instead of a new proprietary
frame, Sudonit is a small module that clips onto the glasses you already own, and moves with
you from one pair to the next. The first version is audio-first: you look, you press a button,
the camera sees what you see, and a voice answers in your ear — while the device still works on
its own without a phone. The phone, when used, only adds the AI; it never takes over. And the
AI is your choice: bring your own key, use a convenient hosted Sudonit service, or point it at
your own home server. You buy the hardware once and it keeps working — no mandatory
subscription, ever. Sudonit isn't trying to out-shine the closed players on polish. It's
competing on the thing they can't offer: **openness, repairability, and true ownership.**

## 3. The Problem — what's wrong with today's smart glasses

- **Locked ecosystems.** You're inside one company's walled garden — their frames, their app,
  their cloud, their rules — with little room to choose differently.
- **No repairability.** Sealed, glued, and disposable. A dead battery or a small fault can mean
  a dead product, because you were never meant to open it.
- **No real ownership.** You paid for it, but you don't control it. Features can change, depend
  on a server staying online, or require a login that isn't really yours.
- **Vendor lock-in.** Your glasses, your data, and your assistant are tied to one company. If
  they raise the price, change the terms, or shut the service down, you're stuck.
- **Proprietary AI.** One assistant, one provider, one set of privacy terms — take it or leave
  it. You can't bring your own model or run it yourself.
- **You must abandon your own glasses.** The frames you already love and wear every day aren't
  invited — you have to buy *theirs*.

The result: the most personal computer many people will ever own — one that sits on your face
and sees what you see — is also the one they understand and control the least.

## 4. The Sudonit Approach

- **Autonomy-first.** The glasses are a standalone device. The phone, the cloud, and the AI
  *extend* it; they never replace it. It stays useful with no phone present.
- **Modularity.** Built from replaceable parts — batteries, audio, microphones, displays, and
  future upgrades — instead of one sealed block. Modularity is a feature, not a compromise.
- **Repairability.** Designed to be opened and fixed. The battery is a service part, modules
  separate, and the clip is a cheap, reprintable piece.
- **User ownership.** Buy it once and it's yours — including the freedom to repair, modify, and
  keep it across different frames.
- **Provider-agnostic AI.** Never locked to one AI vendor. Use your own key, a hosted Sudonit
  service, or your own server — your choice, and it's swappable.

In one line: **a device that works on its own, that you own, that you can fix, and that you can
point at any AI you like.**

## 5. Product Overview — the physical idea

**Sudonit is a MODULE, not a proprietary frame.** This is the single most important thing to
communicate.

- **Clip-on.** A small pod clamps onto the temple arm of the glasses you already own. There is
  **no Sudonit frame to buy** — and that's on purpose.
- **Move it between glasses.** Sunglasses today, reading glasses tomorrow, your nice frames
  tonight — the same module comes with you. Portability is a core part of the idea.
- **Balanced design.** The pod is laid out so the heaviest part — the battery — sits behind the
  ear, balancing the camera at the front so it doesn't slide down your nose. The aim is a
  module light enough to forget.
- **All hardware stays on the head.** Camera, computer, battery, and speaker all live in the
  pod on your glasses. **No battery pack in your pocket, no wire down your neck.**

The long-term look is deliberately understated: **if you're not looking closely, you shouldn't
notice the technology.** Normal glasses, quietly smarter — not a cyberpunk visor.

## 6. Current V1 — honest and unembellished

V1 is **the honest prototype.** It is audio-first and aimed at makers and early adopters who
are comfortable with an open, hands-on device. Describe it plainly; do not oversell it.

**The core experience — look, ask, hear:**
1. Press the button on the pod.
2. The camera captures what's in front of you.
3. The image goes over Wi‑Fi to the phone, which forwards it to your chosen AI.
4. The AI describes or answers.
5. The answer is spoken back through the pod's speaker, into your ear.

And with no phone present, the device is still *alive on its own*: it keeps time and holds its
settings — a standalone wearable the phone makes smarter, not a dead accessory.

- **What exists today:** the look-and-ask-and-hear loop; an autonomous baseline (local clock,
  settings, on-demand capture); audio output; a Wi‑Fi link to the phone; one-button control; a
  clip-on bracket onto existing glasses; runs from USB or a small battery.
- **What's experimental in V1:** a near-eye display / heads-up text. V1 is valid and complete
  as an **audio-only** device; treat any screen as experimental and a real feature in V2.
- **What's planned (later, not V1):** an integrated enclosure, all-day battery, a microphone /
  voice trigger, a polished companion app, and a proper wearable display.
- **What's intentionally excluded from V1:** on-device heavy AI, always-listening wake-word, a
  finished consumer enclosure, and a finished mobile app (early demos use a laptop as the
  "phone brain"). These are deliberate omissions, not oversights — V1's job is to prove the
  core loop openly and cheaply.

**Tone for the site:** confident about the *idea*, honest that V1 is an early open prototype.

## 7. Future Vision — V2, V3, and beyond

Each version is a complete, useful product for *its* audience, not a teaser for the next. The
guiding line: **V1 is for us, V2 is for enthusiasts, V3 is for normal people.** Nothing in V1
is thrown away to get to V3 — the device improves while ownership, autonomy, and privacy stay
constant. *(Use only this established direction; do not invent specific new products or specs.)*

- **V2 — the enthusiast product.** Everything in V1, but integrated: no loose wires, a real
  enclosure, roughly all-day battery, and pairing that behaves like a normal accessory. Adds a
  **wearable display worth keeping on** (notifications, AI answers, navigation, live
  transcription), a microphone, a proper companion app, and a genuinely modular, repairable
  build.
- **V3 — the normal-person product.** Looks like ordinary glasses (or a barely-there clip),
  all-day battery, durable. The display "disappears" — bright enough for daylight, invisible to
  onlookers, glanceable. A polished, privacy-respecting assistant experience and an open
  platform for third-party add-ons.
- **Long-term direction.** Steadily **more local capability and more independence over time** —
  better displays, better optics, and stronger processors, growing toward much more capable
  compute as the device does more on its own. The constant across every version: **open,
  modular, repairable, and yours.**

## 8. AI Philosophy

Sudonit doesn't run large AI on the glasses themselves — that genuinely exceeds a tiny
wearable. Instead the phone brokers the request to an AI of **your** choosing. You're never
locked in:

- **Use your own API key.** Bring your own AI account; requests go to the provider you picked.
  Maximum control, ideal for makers and the privacy-minded.
- **Use hosted Sudonit AI.** A convenient managed option for people who don't want to obtain or
  manage a key. *(Planned as an optional paid service — see Business Model in the FAQ; present
  it as a direction, not a shipping product.)*
- **Use your own server (future).** Point Sudonit at a local or self-hosted model for full
  control of your data.
- **Provider-agnostic by design.** Sudonit never depends on a single AI vendor; the provider is
  replaceable, and none is forced on you.

The headline: **the intelligence is your choice, and you can change it.**

## 9. Privacy Philosophy

Sudonit is **privacy-friendly, not cloud-free** — and the distinction is the whole point.
Cloud AI is *allowed* because it's genuinely useful, but **where your data and your AI live is
always your choice.**

- **What Sudonit controls / promises:** your data belongs to you; no unnecessary cloud storage;
  no permanent retention of your images; no selling of user data; and a visible, honest device
  (you and the people around you can tell it's there).
- **What Sudonit does not control:** if you choose a third-party AI provider with your own key,
  that provider's terms apply to that request — Sudonit doesn't stand between you and the
  vendor you chose. You can avoid this entirely by using a hosted Sudonit option or, in the
  future, your own server.
- **How cloud AI fits a privacy-friendly model:** the device does as much as possible locally,
  and only the genuinely-too-heavy part — AI inference — leaves the device, to a destination
  *you* pick. Privacy here means **choice and control**, not pretending the cloud doesn't
  exist.

## 10. Modularity Ecosystem

Sudonit is meant to be a **modular hardware platform**, not a single fixed gadget — and over
time that opens an ecosystem around the module:

- **Custom batteries.** Different capacities and adapters for different needs and wear times.
- **Replacement parts.** The battery, the clip, and other wear items are made to be swapped,
  not thrown away with the whole device.
- **Printable accessories.** Community-designed, 3D-printable add-ons and mounts.
- **Custom mounts.** Clips sized for different frame styles, so the module fits the glasses you
  actually own.
- **Community hardware.** Makers building and sharing their own components on an open platform.
- **Official hardware.** A first-party catalog of parts and upgrades.

**Why it matters:** modularity is what makes the device *yours for the long run* — repair
instead of replace, upgrade one piece instead of buying a whole new product, and adapt it to
your glasses and your needs. It turns a gadget into a platform, and a platform into something a
community can build on.

## 11. Why Open Hardware Matters

Sudonit's north star is the **ThinkPad** — not the shiniest machine, but the one you can open,
fix, upgrade, and rely on for years because *you* are in control. Applied to glasses:

- **It sits on your face and sees what you see.** A device that intimate should be one you can
  inspect and trust, not a black box.
- **It should outlive a company's roadmap.** Open and repairable means it keeps working even if
  a vendor changes terms, raises prices, or walks away.
- **You can make it yours.** Modify the software, swap parts, print accessories, point it at
  your own AI — the device adapts to you, not the other way around.
- **A community can improve it.** Open hardware and open firmware let other people fix, extend,
  and build on it — value the original maker never has to ship alone.

Why a user cares, in one line: **the most personal computer you'll own shouldn't be the one you
understand and control the least.**

## 12. Frequently Asked Questions

**About the product**

1. **What exactly is Sudonit?** A small module that clips onto glasses you already own and makes
   them smart — camera, speaker, Wi‑Fi, a tiny computer, and a button. Look, press, and hear an
   AI answer.
2. **Is it a pair of glasses?** No. It's a *module* that attaches to your existing frames.
   There's no Sudonit frame to buy.
3. **Do I have to buy special frames?** No — that's the whole point. You keep your own glasses.
4. **Can I move it between different glasses?** Yes. Sunglasses, reading glasses, your nice
   frames — move the same module between them.
5. **Does it have a screen?** V1 is audio-first; a display is experimental in V1 and becomes a
   real feature in later versions. You hear answers rather than read them.
6. **How do I interact with it?** One button (look at something and press), with your phone
   providing the AI behind the scenes.
7. **Is there a battery pack in my pocket?** No. All the hardware stays on the glasses — no
   pocket box, no wire down your neck.
8. **Will it be heavy or slide off my nose?** It's designed so the battery sits behind the ear
   to balance the camera at the front. The goal is a module light enough to forget.
9. **Does it work without my phone?** Yes, for its own functions (like keeping time and holding
   settings). The phone is needed for the AI part, because heavy AI doesn't run on the device.
10. **Can people tell I'm wearing it?** Today's prototype is visible and deliberately honest
    about its camera. The long-term design goal is to be subtle — "if you're not looking
    closely, you shouldn't notice it."

**Comparisons**

11. **Why not just buy Ray-Ban Meta?** If you want a polished, sealed product and you're happy
    inside one company's frames, cloud, and AI, that's a fine choice. Sudonit is for people who
    want the opposite: keep your own glasses, own and repair the hardware, and choose your own
    AI. We compete on openness and ownership, not on polish.
12. **Is Sudonit trying to beat Ray-Ban Meta?** Not on their terms. We're not chasing the
    glossiest closed gadget — we're building the open, repairable, owner-controlled alternative.
13. **How is this different from other smart glasses?** Three ways: it's a portable module (not
    a fixed frame), it's open and repairable (not sealed), and the AI is your choice (not locked
    to one vendor).

**AI and data**

14. **Why use my own AI key?** So you're in control — your account, your provider, your terms —
    instead of being locked to one company's assistant.
15. **What if I don't want to manage an API key?** A hosted Sudonit AI option is planned so you
    can just use it without dealing with keys.
16. **Can I run the AI on my own server?** That's a supported future direction — point Sudonit
    at your own/self-hosted model for full data control.
17. **Why not put everything in the cloud?** Because forcing everything through one cloud means
    less ownership, less privacy, and a device that stops working when the service does. Sudonit
    keeps what it can local and lets *you* choose where the heavy AI runs.
18. **Is Sudonit private?** It's privacy-friendly: your data is yours, no unnecessary storage,
    no permanent image retention, no selling your data. "Privacy-friendly" means *you choose*
    where your data and AI live — not that the cloud is banned.
19. **If I use a third-party AI with my own key, who sees my data?** That request goes to the
    provider you chose, under their terms. To avoid third parties entirely, use a hosted Sudonit
    option or your own server.
20. **Does it record all the time?** No. It's a press-to-ask device, not an always-on recorder.
    Always-listening features are deliberately excluded from V1.

**Ownership, repair, and modification**

21. **Do I actually own it?** Yes — buy the hardware and it's yours, including the right to
    repair, modify, and keep it.
22. **Can I repair it?** Yes. It's designed to be opened — the battery is a service part,
    modules separate, and the clip is a cheap, reprintable piece.
23. **Can I replace the battery?** Yes — it's meant to be swapped, not glued in.
24. **Can I modify it?** Yes. Open firmware and open hardware mean you can change the software
    and swap parts.
25. **Can I 3D-print my own accessories?** That's part of the long-term vision — community and
    official printable mounts and add-ons.
26. **Will it still work if the company disappears?** That's a core reason it's open and
    repairable — it shouldn't depend on any one company staying online to keep functioning.

**Pricing and business model**

27. **Will there be a subscription?** No mandatory subscription. You buy the hardware and it
    keeps working. Optional paid services (like hosted AI) add convenience on top.
28. **What do I pay for, then?** The hardware itself. Optional extras — hosted AI access, a
    model library, cloud sync, premium support — are conveniences, never a gate to using the
    device.
29. **How much does it cost?** Pricing isn't finalized. (Don't quote a retail price on the site
    until it's set.)
30. **When can I buy it?** It's an early, open project; there's no launch date yet. The honest
    framing is "in development."

**Getting involved**

31. **Is it open source?** Yes — open firmware and documentation, built to be modified and
    extended.
32. **Can I contribute?** Yes. Makers, designers, and developers can help with hardware,
    software, accessories, and documentation. (See the Contributor section.)
33. **Who is this for right now?** Honestly, makers and early adopters comfortable with an
    open, hands-on prototype. It grows toward enthusiasts and then everyday wearers.
34. **What's the catch with V1?** It's an early prototype: audio-first, hand-built, no polished
    enclosure or app yet. The idea is proven openly and cheaply first, then refined.
