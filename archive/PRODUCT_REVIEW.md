# PRODUCT_REVIEW.md

A ruthless first-person product review, written as if Sudonit V1 already worked exactly
as designed — and I had to decide whether to keep wearing it. No charity.

---

## Would you personally use Sudonit?

**As a developer/tinkerer: for a weekend, yes. As a daily device: no.** I'd love building
and showing it. I would not reach for it on the way out the door over the phone already in
my pocket or the Ray-Ban Meta on the shelf.

## Why (the honest pull)

- **The idea is genuinely cool**: open, hackable, your own AI key, clips onto the glasses I
  already wear. For someone who likes owning their stack, that's real.
- **The autonomy stance is right**: a device that still tells the time and holds my notes
  when my phone is dead is a better *device* than a dumb peripheral.
- **"Look and ask" is a real desire** — sometimes I do want to point at a thing and get an
  answer without pulling out a phone.

## Why not (the stronger push away)

- **It's a worse version of something that already exists.** Ray-Ban Meta does look-and-ask
  cheaper to own, prettier, faster, supported, and certified. "But mine is open" wins my
  *respect*, not my daily wear.
- **The cost of ownership is a chore**: get an API key, pay per query, keep a phone app
  alive, stay on a known Wi-Fi, charge a behind-the-ear battery that lasts an hour.
- **It looks like a gadget taped to my glasses**, because it is. I won't wear a conspicuous
  camera-clip into a café.
- **The latency tax**: a 2–6 second wait for every answer kills the "magic" by the third
  use. Magic has to be fast.
- **The display, if any, is a colorless 256×64 sliver I have to learn a one-button language
  to drive.** That's a puzzle, not a convenience.

## What feels exciting

- **Ownership + privacy by construction** (your key, your data, no vendor cloud).
- **Clip-on-to-existing-glasses** as a *concept* — not buying a whole new frame.
- **Autonomy-first** — it's a real device, not a screen for the phone.
- **The openness** — I can change anything. For the right person that's the entire appeal.

## What feels gimmicky

- **The near-eye HUD.** On V1 hardware it's a dim, tiny, monochrome novelty; "AR" it is not.
- **On-device Gallery / multiple clock faces / a settings registry** — embedded-GUI
  flexing for a device whose job is one question.
- **The simulator's slickness** — it's beautiful and it shows an experience the real panel
  can't produce, which makes the product *feel* further along than it is.
- **"Competes with Ray-Ban Meta"** — aspirational to the point of gimmick at this stage.

## What features are missing

- **Voice input.** One button cannot be the primary way to ask; the whole loop wants a mic.
- **Speed.** Sub-second perceived response (caching, local pre-processing, lower hops).
- **A real, maintained phone app** on iOS + Android — without it, non-developers can't use it.
- **Battery that lasts a day** and doesn't cook the temple.
- **Confidence/feedback** — a haptic tick so I know a press registered.
- **A trustworthy capture indicator** — for the people *around* me, not just for me.

## What features are unnecessary

- **On-device photo browsing** (I'm holding a phone with a real screen).
- **A full app launcher with 8 apps** on a one-button 256×64 panel.
- **Multiple clock faces / world clock** before the core loop is reliable.
- **The richer parts of the UI runtime** (generic widgets/layout/fonts) ahead of proving
  anyone wants the display at all.

## What would make users abandon it after one week

In rough order of how fast it would happen:

1. **Battery: charging a face-gadget every hour**, and it's warm. Gone by day 3.
2. **Latency + flakiness**: "AI unavailable" or a 5-second pause whenever I'm off my home
   Wi-Fi or my phone slept. Trust gone by day 4.
3. **Social friction**: the first awkward look in public and it stays in the drawer.
4. **Setup/ownership friction**: re-pairing, key management, app babysitting — death by a
   thousand small frictions.
5. **The display is unreadable/unhelpful** outdoors, so I stop glancing at it and it's
   just an awkward speaker.
6. **It does nothing my phone doesn't do better**, so the novelty wears off and the
   inconvenience doesn't.

---

## The reviewer's verdict

Sudonit is **an excellent project and a poor product** — today, and on its current
premise. It would earn a star on GitHub and a "that's awesome" at a hackathon, and it
would lose to a phone within a week of real wear. The path to a *product* is not more
features; it's brutal subtraction (one reliable, fast, voice-driven "ask" loop), honesty
about the display, a real answer to power, and — hardest of all — a reason for a
non-developer to want it that "it's open" cannot supply. As an open platform for makers,
it can be genuinely loved. As a thing normal people buy and wear, it is not close, and no
amount of polish on the parts that already work will get it there.
